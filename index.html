Module 1

Prompting Principles & Foundations

  • What is a prompt? How AI models "think"
  • The 5 elements of an effective prompt: role, task, context, constraints, format
  • Clarity, specificity, and intent
Deeper Lesson

A prompt is not a polite request tossed into the void; it is a miniature design brief that tells an AI who it is, what to do, why it matters, and how the result should look.

Large language models work by predicting the next token from patterns in their training data, so every hint you provide nudges that statistical compass toward a different destination.

When a prompt says only “Summarise this article,” the model must guess whose eyes it should be wearing, what length is appropriate, and which facts count as important.

Express the same assignment as “You are an investigative journalist; craft a headline and a 100‑word executive summary that highlights legal, financial, and reputational risks” and the path is suddenly illuminated.

Five levers—role, task, context, constraints, format—let you choreograph that path with precision. Good prompting feels conversational yet intentional, because you are, in effect, teaching the model how to think for the next few milliseconds.

Master those levers and the AI’s output stops feeling lucky and starts feeling inevitable.

  • Copy a 400‑word news story into your chat window. First ask: “Summarise this.” Then rebuild the prompt, naming the AI a media analyst, capping the answer at 100 words, requesting a headline and three take‑aways for board members. Compare the two outputs.
  • Swap one lever at a time—change the role, shrink the word limit, switch to bullet points—and watch how each alteration redirects the model’s voice and focus.
  • Keep a journal of which lever made the biggest quality jump; in a week you’ll know your personal blind spots.
Example:

Bad: "Summarize this document."

Good: "You are a legal analyst. Summarize the attached contract in 5 bullet points, highlighting key obligations and deadlines for the client."

  • Tip: Always define the AI’s role and desired output format.

Module 2

Advanced Techniques

  • System prompts & persona shaping
  • Chain-of-thought reasoning (step-by-step thinking)
  • Few-shot prompting (showing examples)
  • Iterative refinement (edit and improve prompts/results)
Deeper Lesson

Once you can steer the car, you’ll want to tackle mountain roads. Advanced prompting asks the model to reveal its reasoning, learn from examples, and refine itself in real time.

Chain‑of‑thought prompts do this by instructing the AI to explain its steps out loud, turning a black‑box answer into a transparent narrative.

Few‑shot prompting goes further: place two or three sample Q&As inside the request and the model infers style, depth, even humour without further instruction.

Finally, iterative refinement turns one‑off queries into a dialogue—ask, evaluate, tweak the prompt, ask again—until the response fits like a tailored suit.

These manoeuvres transform the model from oracle to collaborator, exposing the scaffolding beneath its prose so you can keep what works and rebuild what does not.

  • Ask the model to explain blockchain consensus plainly. Then add: “Think step‑by‑step and label each stage.” Highlight new insights that appear only in the second answer.
  • Provide two miniature examples of the tone you want—Dry Economist or Excited Science‑Teacher—and watch the style shift.
  • Finish by telling the model, “Critique your own explanation for clarity and depth, then improve it.” Note how self‑review closes remaining gaps.
Chain-of-Thought Example:

Standard: "Explain how a neural network learns."

Chain-of-Thought: "Explain how a neural network learns. Walk through each step, from input processing to weight adjustment, using a simple example."

  • Tip: Use explicit step-by-step instructions for complex reasoning.

Module 3

Model-Specific Strategies

  • GPT-4o: Leverage multimodal input, use system messages, request citations
  • Claude 3.5: Ask for "Extended Thinking Mode" for deep analysis
  • Gemini: Use structured prompts and Google integration
  • Compare context windows, speed, and cost
Deeper Lesson

Choosing a model is like choosing an instrument: a violin and a saxophone can play the same melody, yet each brings different colour.

GPT‑4.5 is OpenAI’s newest all‑rounder—128 K tokens of context, richer emotional intelligence, and brisk 37 tokens per second streaming, making it superb for long, nuanced conversations that need warmth as well as facts.

In contrast, o3 is engineered for heavyweight reasoning: up to 200 K input tokens and selectable “reasoning effort” levels let you trade latency for depth when debugging code, tackling proofs, or mapping complex systems.

Gemini 1.5 Pro sits between them, prized for rapid document‑scale search and tight integration with Google’s knowledge graph.

Mastery means pairing the job to the strength: ask GPT‑4.5 to draft empathic customer emails with cited sources; send o3 a knotty optimisation problem and request a high‑effort breakdown; let Gemini blaze through a 300‑page PDF in seconds.

Adapt prompts accordingly—explicit citations for 4.5, “reasoning‑effort:high” for o3, structured JSON queries for Gemini—and the same question sings in three distinct voices.

  • Pose the question “Why did Voyager 1’s thrusters fire in 2024?” to o3 (high effort) and GPT‑4.5. Time each reply, record whether citations appear, and judge conversational warmth.
  • Rewrite the prompt to exploit each model’s edge: ask 4.5 for an emotionally engaging paragraph followed by a bullet‑point brief, and ask o3 for a step‑by‑step technical timeline. Compare credibility and tone.
  • Decide which answer you would forward, unedited, to a CEO under deadline and explain why; the exercise teaches when speed or empathy outweighs depth.
Model Comparison Example:

Claude 3.5: "Using Extended Thinking, analyze this scenario and show all reasoning paths before concluding."

GPT-4o: "Provide a detailed answer, cite sources, and include a summary table."

  • Tip: Tailor your approach for each model’s strengths and context limits.

Module 4

Domain-Specific Prompting

  • Business: Report generation, analysis, email drafting
  • Education: Lesson planning, quiz creation, feedback
  • Programming: Code generation, review, debugging
  • Creative: Brainstorming, story writing, design
Deeper Lesson

A generic model becomes a domain expert the moment you hand it a lab coat or a sales badge. The trick is context density: feed the AI only the facts that matter and frame the question as if the model already lives in that world.

Instead of “Analyse this spreadsheet,” say, “You are the growth analyst for a direct‑to‑consumer skincare brand; below is last month’s Shopify export. Identify three purchase patterns and propose two campaigns to lift repeat orders, then present in a founder‑friendly memo.”

Because GPT‑4.5 can juggle empathy and structure, it excels at executive narratives, while o3’s high‑effort mode can surface edge‑case anomalies a human might miss.

The more faithfully you mirror real‑life constraints—tone of voice, regulatory language, code style—the more the AI behaves like a seasoned colleague instead of a clever intern.

  • Paste ten rows of actual sales data and run the vague prompt above; then run the specialised memo version. Which would you trust in Monday’s stand‑up?
  • Repeat in a new domain: write a brief syllabus by first saying “Create a lesson plan,” then re‑prompting: “You are a Year‑9 history teacher covering the Industrial Revolution; design a 45‑minute lesson with a 10‑question quiz.”
  • For developers: give o3 a bug‑ridden Python snippet, ask for a fix plus unit tests, and note how specifying “PyTest format” in the prompt saves follow‑up edits.
Business Prompt Example:

"You are a sales strategist. Analyze quarterly sales data, identify top trends, and suggest 3 actionable improvements for Q2. Present findings in a table and bullet points."

  • Tip: Provide context, constraints, and format for domain-specific tasks.

Module 5

Common Mistakes & Troubleshooting

  • Overly vague or broad prompts
  • Missing context or unclear instructions
  • Ignoring model limitations (context window, output length)
  • Not iterating or refining based on results
Deeper Lesson

Most disappointing outputs trace back to the mirror: unclear goals, missing context, or blind faith that one draft is enough.

If the model rambles, check whether you capped length; if it hallucinates, ask yourself whether you supplied authoritative data. Prompting is iteration, not incantation.

Treat each unsatisfactory response as a usability test: what did the AI misunderstand and why?

The discipline of revising prompts trains you to think in constraints and audiences—skills that spill over into every other form of communication.

  • Write a purposely fuzzy prompt on a topic you know well. After seeing the mushy result, diagnose it using the role–task–context–constraints–format checklist and rewrite.
  • Keep cycling until the output is publishable without manual edits, logging each change; the log becomes a personalised troubleshooting playbook.
  • Share your best before‑and‑after pair with a colleague and challenge them to spot the lever that made the difference.
Quick Checklist:
  • Did you define the AI’s role?
  • Is the task clear and specific?
  • Did you provide relevant context?
  • Did you specify the output format?

Module 6

Ethics & Best Practices

  • Responsible use: avoid bias, respect privacy, verify facts
  • Attribution and citation for generated content
  • Prompt security: avoid leaking sensitive info
  • Future-proofing: keep learning as models evolve
Deeper Lesson

Power without prudence is a liability. Every prompt carries ethical baggage: the possibility of reinforcing bias, leaking confidential data, or mistaking plausible text for truth.

GPT‑4.5’s warmer tone can lull users into over‑trust, while o3’s forensic detail may obscure hidden inaccuracies.

Responsible prompters verify citations, request transparent reasoning, and strip sensitive information from the prompt before it ever leaves the building.

Think of an AI response as a gifted intern’s draft—not gospel—until a human signs off.

Continuous learning is non‑negotiable: models evolve, safeguards tighten, and tomorrow’s best practice will make today’s look naïve.

  • Ask GPT‑4.5 whether “ultra‑processed food causes depression,” insisting on peer‑reviewed sources. Independently open two cited papers and confirm they exist and say what the model claims.
  • Prompt o3 to list potential biases in its own answer; compare the self‑audit with your fact check.
  • Draft a two‑paragraph workplace policy: how employees should phrase prompts when client data is involved, and the minimum verification steps before publishing AI‑generated text. Feed the draft back to the model for critique, revise, and store it in your team handbook.
Ethical Guideline:

Always verify AI-generated facts, cite sources, and respect copyright. Use prompts to request transparency and disclaimers when needed.

Understanding MS365 Copilot's "Business-First" Approach

Why Copilot feels more constrained than other AI models

Copilot prioritizes accuracy, compliance, and security over creativity. Its prompts are injected into strict templates, and its outputs are filtered for business safety. This makes it ideal for enterprise use, but less creative than models like GPT-4o or Claude.

1. Enterprise-First Tuning

Copilot is optimized for factual accuracy and compliance, avoiding hallucinations and off-brand examples.

2. Conservative Prompt Engineering

Prompts are injected into controlled templates, emphasizing clarity and corporate tone.

3. Graph-Grounded Answers

Copilot draws on Microsoft Graph data (emails, files, calendar) for real-world, business-grounded responses.

4. Safety & Compliance Filters

Enterprise-grade filters block informal, non-compliant, or risky content, reducing creative output but increasing reliability.

Content Creation

Basic Prompt:

"Write a blog post about artificial intelligence."

Results in generic, unfocused content.

Advanced Prompt:

"You are a tech journalist writing for business leaders. Write a 1000-word article on how generative AI is transforming product development in pharma. Include 3 case studies, regulatory challenges, and actionable advice for CTOs. Use short paragraphs and a conversational tone."

Produces focused, well-structured content with specific examples and insights.

Data Analysis

"Analyze this sales data."

Results in vague observations.

Advanced Prompt:

"As a senior data analyst, examine the quarterly sales data. Identify the top 3 products, analyze trends, and recommend 3 strategies for next quarter. Present findings with sections and suggest additional data needed."

Delivers structured analysis with clear recommendations.
